## AI Is a Team Sport

The most sophisticated AI technology in the world fails without the right people deploying, managing, and collaborating with it. Yet most AI adoption discussions focus exclusively on technology while treating the human side as an afterthought. The organizations that extract the most value from AI are those that invest as deliberately in people and team structures as they do in models and infrastructure.

Deloitte's 2024 State of AI in the Enterprise survey found that talent shortages are the number one barrier to AI adoption, cited by 47% of organizations — ahead of data quality (38%), technology complexity (33%), and budget constraints (28%).

## The Core AI Team: Roles You Actually Need

An effective AI team doesn't require an army of PhDs. It requires the right combination of skills organized around clear responsibilities:

### AI/ML Engineers

These are the builders — the people who develop, train, deploy, and maintain AI models. They write the code that connects models to data, build inference pipelines, and optimize performance. A strong ML engineer understands both the theoretical foundations of machine learning and the practical engineering required to make models work in production.

**What to look for:** Experience deploying models to production (not just notebooks), familiarity with cloud ML platforms, and the pragmatism to choose simple solutions when they work.

### Data Engineers

Data engineers build and maintain the pipelines that feed data to AI models. They design data architectures, manage ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) processes, ensure data quality, and make data accessible to the rest of the team. Without strong data engineering, even the best models starve.

**What to look for:** Experience with modern data stack tools (dbt, Airflow, Spark), familiarity with both batch and streaming data architectures, and a relentless focus on data quality.

### Product Managers (AI-Focused)

AI product managers translate business needs into AI requirements and AI capabilities into business value. They define success metrics, prioritize use cases, manage stakeholder expectations, and make the critical "ship it or iterate" decisions that determine whether AI projects deliver value or run indefinitely.

**What to look for:** Someone who understands both business strategy and AI capabilities well enough to have informed conversations with both executives and engineers. They don't need to code, but they need to understand what models can and cannot do.

### Domain Experts

Domain experts are the people who deeply understand the business process the AI is automating or augmenting. A fraud detection model built without fraud analysts is guessing. A clinical decision support tool built without clinicians is dangerous. Domain experts validate model outputs, identify edge cases, and provide the training data that makes models accurate.

**What to look for:** Deep process knowledge, willingness to collaborate with technologists, and the patience to review model outputs systematically.

### AI Ethics and Governance Leads

As AI systems make or influence consequential decisions, organizations need people who evaluate fairness, bias, privacy, and compliance. This role ensures AI deployments meet regulatory requirements, organizational values, and societal expectations.

## Team Structures That Work

How you organize your AI team matters as much as who is on it. Three models dominate:

### Centralized AI Center of Excellence

A single team serves the entire organization, building AI capabilities that every department can use. This model ensures consistency, avoids duplicate effort, and concentrates scarce talent.

**Best for:** Organizations in the early stages of AI adoption that need to build foundational capabilities. Procter & Gamble established a centralized AI hub that developed reusable tools and frameworks before distributing AI capabilities to business units.

**Risk:** The centralized team becomes a bottleneck, and departments wait months for their AI projects to reach the front of the queue.

### Embedded AI Teams

AI specialists are distributed into business units — marketing, operations, finance — where they work alongside domain experts on unit-specific problems. This model produces faster, more contextually relevant AI applications.

**Best for:** Organizations with mature data infrastructure and enough AI talent to distribute. Spotify embeds ML engineers within product squads so that AI development is tightly coupled with product development, reducing handoff friction.

**Risk:** Without central coordination, teams reinvent wheels, adopt incompatible tools, and create maintenance nightmares.

### Hub-and-Spoke Model

A central AI team sets standards, builds shared infrastructure, and provides consulting support, while embedded specialists in business units execute projects using those shared resources. This combines the consistency of centralization with the responsiveness of embedding.

**Best for:** Organizations scaling from initial AI pilots to enterprise-wide adoption. Google operates this model, with DeepMind and Google AI providing foundational research while product teams integrate AI into specific applications.

## Upskilling Your Existing Workforce

You cannot hire your way out of the AI talent gap — there simply aren't enough experienced AI practitioners to staff every organization. The organizations winning the talent war are the ones investing in upskilling existing employees.

### Tiered Training Programs

**AI literacy for all employees** — Every employee should understand what AI can and cannot do, how to collaborate with AI tools, and how to evaluate AI-generated outputs. This is not a technology training — it's a business capability. Amazon committed $1.2 billion to upskilling 300,000 employees by 2025, including AI and machine learning fundamentals for non-technical roles.

**AI fluency for managers** — Managers need to understand how to identify AI opportunities in their workflows, how to evaluate AI vendor claims, and how to manage teams that include AI tools. This includes recognizing when a task is a good AI candidate and when it isn't.

**AI proficiency for technical staff** — Developers, data analysts, and IT professionals need hands-on training with AI development tools, prompt engineering, model evaluation, and deployment practices. Many organizations partner with platforms like Coursera, Udacity, or internal academies to deliver this training.

### The Prompt Engineering Gap

One of the most immediately valuable and underappreciated skills is prompt engineering — the ability to communicate effectively with AI models to get reliable, high-quality outputs. Prompt engineering is not a developer skill exclusively; it is a communication skill that benefits anyone who uses AI tools in their work.

Organizations that invest in prompt engineering training for business users — sales teams, analysts, customer service representatives, project managers — see faster AI adoption and higher satisfaction with AI tools. BCG's 2024 research found that consultants trained in effective AI collaboration were 40% more productive than untrained peers using the same tools.

## Change Management: The Human Side of AI Adoption

Technology adoption fails when people resist it. AI adoption triggers specific anxieties — job displacement, loss of autonomy, distrust of algorithmic decisions — that require deliberate change management:

- **Communicate the "why" before the "what"** — Before introducing AI tools, explain the business rationale and how AI changes (not eliminates) roles
- **Involve end users in design** — People support systems they helped shape. Include frontline workers in AI pilot design and feedback loops
- **Celebrate augmentation, not replacement** — Frame AI as a tool that handles routine work so people can focus on judgment, creativity, and relationship-building
- **Provide psychological safety** — Create environments where employees can express concerns about AI without fear of being labeled as resistant to change

> **Key takeaway:** AI adoption succeeds or fails based on people, not technology. Build teams with clear roles, choose a team structure that matches your organizational maturity, invest in tiered upskilling, and treat change management as a first-class project — not an afterthought.
