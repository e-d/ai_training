## What AI Actually Means

Artificial intelligence is the science of building systems that can perform tasks which normally require human intelligence — recognizing images, understanding language, making decisions, and solving problems. At its core, AI is about creating software that can learn from data rather than following only hard-coded rules.

That distinction matters for business leaders. Traditional software does exactly what a programmer tells it to do. AI systems improve their performance as they process more data, which means they can handle ambiguity, adapt to new patterns, and scale decision-making in ways that rule-based programs cannot.

## A Brief History: From Theory to Transformation

The idea of intelligent machines dates back to 1950, when mathematician Alan Turing published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" and proposed what became known as the Turing Test — a benchmark for whether a machine could exhibit behavior indistinguishable from a human.

The term "artificial intelligence" was coined in 1956 at the Dartmouth Conference, where researchers John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, and others laid the groundwork for AI as a field of study. Early progress was promising but limited by computing power and available data.

The field went through two periods known as "AI winters" — the late 1970s and late 1980s — where funding dried up because the technology couldn't deliver on its ambitious promises. What changed everything was the convergence of three forces in the 2010s:

- **Massive datasets** from the internet, smartphones, and IoT devices
- **Powerful GPUs** that could process data thousands of times faster than traditional CPUs
- **Algorithmic breakthroughs** in neural networks and deep learning

By 2020, AI had moved from research labs into everyday business operations. Today, AI systems process loan applications, diagnose diseases, translate languages in real time, and generate human-quality text and images.

## Three Types of AI

Researchers typically classify AI into three categories based on capability:

### Narrow AI (What Exists Today)

Also called "weak AI," narrow AI is designed for a specific task. Every AI system in production today — from Siri to self-driving car software — is narrow AI. It can outperform humans within its defined scope but cannot generalize beyond it. A chess-playing AI cannot suddenly help you write a marketing plan.

### General AI (The Next Frontier)

Artificial general intelligence (AGI) would match human-level reasoning across any intellectual task. AGI doesn't exist yet, but it drives much of the research at labs like OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Anthropic. Estimates for when AGI might arrive range from the next few years to several decades — there is no consensus.

### Superintelligent AI (Theoretical)

Superintelligent AI would surpass human cognitive abilities in every domain. This remains a theoretical concept, but it figures prominently in policy discussions about AI safety and long-term risk.

> **Key takeaway:** As a business leader, your focus should be on narrow AI — the technology that is deployable, proven, and delivering ROI today. Understanding the broader trajectory helps you plan strategically, but the immediate opportunity is in applied, task-specific AI.

## Why Business Leaders Should Care Now

AI is no longer an emerging technology — it is a competitive differentiator. According to McKinsey's 2025 Global Survey on AI, 72% of organizations have adopted AI in at least one business function, up from 55% in 2023. Companies that delay adoption risk falling behind competitors who are using AI to:

- **Reduce costs** through process automation and operational efficiency
- **Improve customer experience** with personalization and faster response times
- **Make better decisions** by analyzing data at a scale no human team can match

## Real-World Examples

**Netflix** uses AI recommendation algorithms that analyze viewing patterns across 280+ million subscribers, driving an estimated 80% of the content watched on the platform. This personalization engine saves the company roughly $1 billion per year in customer retention.

**Google Search** processes over 8.5 billion queries per day using AI models that understand natural language, rank results by relevance, and continuously learn from user behavior. The 2023 introduction of AI-powered Search Generative Experience marked a shift from link-based results to conversational answers.

**JPMorgan Chase** deploys AI across fraud detection, contract analysis, and customer service. Their COiN platform uses natural language processing to review commercial loan agreements — work that previously required 360,000 hours of lawyer time annually — in seconds.
