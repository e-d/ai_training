## Start with the Problem, Not the Technology

The most common mistake organizations make with AI is falling in love with a tool before identifying the problem it should solve. A 2024 RAND Corporation study found that roughly 80% of AI projects fail, and the leading causes aren't technical — they're organizational: unclear objectives, poor data quality, and lack of executive sponsorship. This lesson provides a practical six-step framework for launching your first AI initiative.

## Step 1: Identify a Business Problem Worth Solving

Start by listing processes that are data-rich, repetitive, and currently bottlenecked by human bandwidth. Good candidates include customer inquiry routing, invoice processing, demand forecasting, and quality inspection. The problem should be specific enough to measure — "improve customer experience" is too vague, but "reduce average support ticket resolution time from 4 hours to 1 hour" gives you a clear target.

## Step 2: Assess Your Data Readiness

AI runs on data, and most organizations overestimate how ready theirs is. Before committing to a project, audit three dimensions: **availability** (do you actually collect the data the model would need?), **quality** (is it clean, consistent, and complete?), and **accessibility** (can your team get to it without navigating months of bureaucratic or technical barriers?). A 2023 Gartner survey found that poor data quality costs organizations an average of $12.9 million per year — cleaning your data often delivers value before the AI model even exists.

## Step 3: Assemble a Cross-Functional Team

AI projects fail when they live exclusively in the IT department. Effective teams combine **data scientists or ML engineers** who build the models, **domain experts** from the business unit who understand the problem deeply, **data engineers** who manage pipelines and infrastructure, and an **executive sponsor** who can secure resources and remove organizational roadblocks. The domain experts are especially critical — they know which edge cases matter and can tell you when a model's output doesn't make sense.

## Step 4: Choose Build vs. Buy vs. Partner

Not every AI project requires building a model from scratch. The decision depends on three factors: how unique your problem is, how proprietary your data advantage needs to be, and how much in-house expertise you have.

- **Buy (off-the-shelf)** — Best for common use cases like document classification, chatbots, or sentiment analysis where established vendors offer proven solutions
- **Partner (managed services)** — Suitable when you have proprietary data but lack ML engineering talent; cloud providers like AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure offer pre-built AI services that you can fine-tune
- **Build (custom)** — Worth the investment when competitive advantage depends on proprietary algorithms trained on your unique data, as with Netflix's recommendation engine or Tesla's self-driving models

## Step 5: Start with a Pilot

Scope your first project to deliver measurable results within 60 to 90 days. Starbucks took this approach when developing Deep Brew, its AI platform — starting with a personalized recommendation engine for the mobile app before expanding to labor scheduling and inventory management across 35,000+ locations. A successful pilot generates internal momentum and gives you the evidence to justify broader investment.

> **Key takeaway:** Resist the urge to boil the ocean. A focused pilot that solves one real problem is worth more than an ambitious roadmap that never delivers.

## Step 6: Measure, Learn, and Iterate

Define success metrics before launch and track them rigorously. Common AI metrics include prediction accuracy, time savings, error rate reduction, and revenue impact. Equally important: monitor for model drift — the gradual degradation of AI performance as real-world data shifts away from the training data. Companies like Uber and Airbnb retrain their core models on weekly or monthly cycles to maintain accuracy.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even well-planned AI initiatives can stumble. Three pitfalls account for most failures:

- **Ignoring change management** — AI changes workflows, and people resist what they don't understand. JPMorgan Chase invested heavily in training programs when deploying its COiN contract-analysis system, which now processes 12,000 commercial credit agreements per year in seconds instead of 360,000 hours of manual review
- **Underestimating data quality** — Garbage in, garbage out is not a cliché in AI — it's a law. Budget 60-80% of project time for data preparation and cleaning
- **Skipping governance** — Without clear policies on who approves model deployment, monitors outputs, and decides when to retrain or retire a model, organizations risk deploying systems that quietly degrade or produce harmful results
