## High-Stakes Prompting: When the Output Has Consequences

Marketing content that's slightly off-tone is a nuisance. A financial analysis with a wrong number or a legal review that misses a critical clause is a liability. Prompt engineering for financial and legal work operates under different rules: precision matters more than creativity, caveats matter more than confidence, and verification is mandatory rather than optional.

This lesson covers the techniques that finance and legal professionals use to extract reliable, auditable AI outputs for high-stakes business decisions.

## Financial Analysis Prompts

### Earnings and Financial Statement Analysis

**Basic prompt (produces generic output):**

"Analyze Apple's latest earnings report."

**Professional-grade prompt:**

"You are a sell-side equity research analyst covering Apple Inc. I'm sharing Apple's Q4 2024 10-Q filing. Analyze it using the following framework: (1) Revenue decomposition — break down revenue by segment and geography. Identify which segments are accelerating and which are decelerating vs. prior quarter and prior year. (2) Margin analysis — calculate and compare gross margin, operating margin, and net margin vs. the prior four quarters. Identify the primary drivers of any margin expansion or compression. (3) Cash flow analysis — compare operating cash flow to net income. Flag any divergence and explain what it might indicate. (4) Forward indicators — identify any language in the MD&A section that signals management's expectations for next quarter. Use exact quotes. Present findings in a structured format with a summary table at the top."

This prompt produces analysis comparable to a junior analyst's first draft — structured, specific, and grounded in the actual filing rather than the model's general knowledge about Apple.

### Financial Modeling Assistance

AI cannot replace a spreadsheet for financial modeling, but it excels at structuring assumptions, checking logic, and drafting formulas.

**Example prompt:**

"I'm building a 3-year DCF model for a SaaS company with the following characteristics: $50M ARR, 30% YoY growth (decelerating 5 points annually), 70% gross margin, 25% operating margin at scale, $15M in annual capex. Help me: (1) List every assumption I need to define, organized by category (revenue, costs, working capital, terminal value). (2) For each assumption, suggest a reasonable range based on SaaS industry benchmarks. (3) Identify which 3 assumptions will have the largest impact on the final valuation — these are where I should focus my sensitivity analysis. Show your reasoning step by step."

### Ratio Analysis and Benchmarking

**Example prompt:**

"Calculate and interpret the following ratios from the attached balance sheet and income statement: Current ratio, Quick ratio, Debt-to-equity, Interest coverage, Return on equity, Asset turnover. For each ratio: (1) Show the calculation with actual numbers, (2) State whether the result is above or below industry median for [specific industry], (3) Explain what the ratio tells us about the company's financial health, (4) Flag any ratios that are trending in a concerning direction compared to the prior year figures I've provided. If you don't have reliable industry benchmarks, say so rather than estimating."

That last instruction — "say so rather than estimating" — is critical for financial prompting. It transforms the AI from a confident-sounding generator into an honest analyst who flags uncertainty.

## Legal Analysis Prompts

### Contract Review

**Example prompt:**

"You are a corporate attorney reviewing a vendor services agreement. Review the attached contract and identify: (1) Termination provisions — what are the notice requirements, termination-for-convenience rights, and termination-for-cause triggers? Quote the relevant sections. (2) Liability and indemnification — what are the liability caps? Are there any unlimited liability carve-outs? Who indemnifies whom, and for what? (3) IP ownership — who owns work product created during the engagement? Are there any license-back provisions? (4) Data handling — what are the data protection obligations? Do they align with GDPR and CCPA requirements? (5) Non-standard terms — flag any provisions that deviate from market-standard terms for this type of agreement. For each issue identified, rate it as Low Risk, Medium Risk, or High Risk, and explain your reasoning. Important: This analysis is for internal review purposes. Do not present findings as legal advice. Flag any provisions where you are uncertain of the interpretation."

Allen & Overy, one of the world's largest law firms, reported that their AI tool Harvey reduced initial contract review time by 50% — but emphasized that every AI-generated review undergoes attorney verification before being shared with clients. The AI accelerates the first pass; it does not replace professional judgment.

### Regulatory Compliance Analysis

**Example prompt:**

"Our company processes health data for insurance clients. We need to ensure compliance with HIPAA, state privacy laws in California (CCPA/CPRA) and New York, and our SOC 2 Type II certification requirements. Review our current data handling policy [attached] and: (1) Identify specific provisions that satisfy each regulatory requirement, (2) Identify gaps — requirements that our policy doesn't address, (3) For each gap, draft recommended policy language that would address the requirement, (4) Rate each gap by severity: Critical (immediate compliance risk), Important (should address within 90 days), or Advisory (best practice improvement). Organize findings in a compliance matrix format."

### Litigation Risk Assessment

**Example prompt:**

"You are outside litigation counsel assessing risk for a potential breach of contract claim. The facts are: [describe situation]. Analyze the following: (1) Elements of the claim — can the opposing party establish each required element? Rate the strength of their position for each element (Strong, Moderate, Weak). (2) Available defenses — identify all potentially applicable defenses and assess their viability. (3) Damages exposure — what categories of damages could be sought, and what is the realistic range? (4) Settlement considerations — what factors weigh in favor of and against early settlement? Present your analysis as a litigation risk memo with a bottom-line risk rating: High, Medium, or Low. Caveat: This is preliminary analysis for internal discussion, not legal advice."

## Critical Safeguards for Financial and Legal Prompting

### The Verification Imperative

Every financial figure and legal citation produced by AI must be verified against primary sources. A 2024 analysis by Thomson Reuters found that AI-generated legal research contained material errors in approximately 15% of cases — often citing real cases with fabricated holdings, or conflating provisions from different jurisdictions.

**Prompting to reduce (not eliminate) errors:**

"When citing specific statutes, regulations, or case law, provide the full citation so I can verify it. If you are not certain that a specific legal authority exists or says what you describe, explicitly state your uncertainty rather than presenting it as fact."

### Audit Trails and Documentation

For regulated industries, the ability to explain how an analysis was produced is as important as the analysis itself. Save your prompt chains, not just the outputs. If a regulator asks how you arrived at a compliance determination, "AI told us" is not an acceptable answer. A documented prompt chain showing the analytical framework, inputs, and step-by-step reasoning is.

### Scope Limitations

Always define what AI-assisted analysis is and is not. Internal first drafts for human review? Yes. Client-facing deliverables without attorney or analyst review? Never. The prompt itself should include scope language:

"This analysis is for internal use as a first draft to be reviewed and refined by [qualified professional]. It should not be treated as final analysis, legal advice, or financial recommendation."

### Confidentiality Protocols

Financial and legal work involves highly sensitive information. Only use enterprise-grade AI tools with appropriate data handling agreements. Never paste client financial data, privileged legal communications, or material non-public information into consumer AI tools.

> **Key takeaway:** Financial and legal prompt engineering demands a different mindset than creative or marketing prompting. Precision, verification, uncertainty flagging, and scope limitations aren't optional extras — they're fundamental requirements. AI accelerates the analytical process but does not replace the professional judgment that makes the final output trustworthy.
