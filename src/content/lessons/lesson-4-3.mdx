## From Strategy to Execution

Plenty of organizations have an AI strategy. Far fewer have a track record of successful AI implementation. A 2024 RAND Corporation study found that approximately 80% of AI projects fail — not because of bad algorithms, but because of poor implementation practices. The gap between strategy and results is where most AI investments die.

This lesson covers the practical frameworks that separate organizations that ship AI from those that stall in perpetual piloting.

## The Implementation Lifecycle

### Phase 1: Problem Selection

The most common implementation mistake is solving the wrong problem. Leaders often choose AI projects based on what sounds impressive ("Let's build a predictive analytics platform!") rather than what creates business value. Effective problem selection follows the ICE framework:

- **Impact** — How much business value will solving this problem create? Quantify it in revenue, cost savings, or time saved
- **Confidence** — How certain are you that AI can solve this problem? Is the task data-rich, repetitive, and pattern-based?
- **Ease** — What is the implementation complexity? Do you have the data, the talent, and the infrastructure?

Score each criterion on a 1-10 scale. Multiply the scores. Prioritize ruthlessly. A project scoring 8-7-3 (high impact, high confidence, low ease) may be worse than one scoring 6-6-8 (moderate impact, moderate confidence, high ease) — because the second will actually get done.

### Phase 2: Pilot Design

A well-designed pilot has five elements:

1. **Clear hypothesis** — "We believe that AI-powered email triage will reduce response time for priority-one customer issues by 40% within 60 days." Not "We want to explore AI for customer service"
2. **Bounded scope** — Apply the solution to one team, one product line, or one region. DHL piloted AI-driven route optimization in a single city before scaling to its global network
3. **Predefined success criteria** — What numbers must improve, by how much, for the pilot to justify scaling? Define these before the pilot starts, not after results come in
4. **Control group** — Measure the AI-assisted group against a comparable group working without AI. Without a control, you cannot attribute results to the technology versus other factors
5. **Time boundary** — 60-90 days for most business pilots. Long enough to generate meaningful data, short enough to maintain organizational attention

### Phase 3: Build and Deploy

This is where technical and organizational execution intersect. Key practices:

**Start with existing data.** The instinct to "collect better data first" delays projects indefinitely. Successful teams build models on available data, demonstrate value, and then invest in improved data collection. Amazon's recommendation engine started with purchase history alone before incorporating browsing behavior, search queries, and demographic data.

**Deploy in the workflow, not beside it.** An AI tool that requires employees to leave their primary application, log into a separate system, and copy-paste data will not be used. Salesforce's Einstein AI succeeds partly because it surfaces predictions inside the CRM interface that salespeople already use daily.

**Plan for the "last mile."** The model is only half the work. Integration, user training, change management, and feedback collection are the other half. Google's internal research found that model accuracy explains about 50% of AI project success — the rest depends on how well the solution fits into existing work patterns.

### Phase 4: Evaluate and Decide

After the pilot, you face one of four decisions:

| Outcome | Action |
|---|---|
| **Met or exceeded success criteria** | Scale — expand to additional teams, regions, or use cases |
| **Showed promise but fell short** | Iterate — adjust the model, the workflow, or the scope, and run another bounded test |
| **Showed no measurable improvement** | Pivot — the problem may not be well-suited for AI, or the approach needs fundamental rethinking |
| **Caused harm or new problems** | Kill — some AI applications create more problems than they solve. Cutting losses early is a sign of discipline, not failure |

The hardest decision is "kill." Organizations that can't kill failing AI projects accumulate technical debt and erode trust. Netflix famously cancels shows based on data regardless of how much was invested — the same discipline should apply to AI pilots.

## Measuring AI Impact: Beyond Accuracy

Technical teams measure AI models by accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 scores. Business leaders need different metrics. An AI model that is 95% accurate but doesn't change any business outcome is a technical success and a business failure.

### The Business Impact Framework

**Tier 1: Operational Metrics** — Direct, measurable changes in the process where AI is deployed
- Processing time reduced (e.g., invoice review from 20 minutes to 3 minutes)
- Error rates decreased (e.g., data entry errors from 5% to 0.8%)
- Throughput increased (e.g., customer inquiries handled per hour up 40%)

**Tier 2: Business Outcome Metrics** — Higher-level results that operational improvements drive
- Revenue impact (e.g., AI-driven upsell recommendations increased average order value by 12%)
- Cost savings (e.g., automated triage reduced support staffing costs by $2.1M annually)
- Customer satisfaction (e.g., NPS increased 8 points after AI-powered personalization)

**Tier 3: Strategic Metrics** — Long-term competitive position changes
- Time-to-market for new products or features
- Employee retention and satisfaction (a proxy for how well AI is augmenting rather than threatening workers)
- Data asset growth (better data feeding better models in a virtuous cycle)

### The Counter-Factual Trap

The biggest measurement error is comparing AI performance to zero rather than to the alternative. An AI chatbot that resolves 70% of customer inquiries sounds impressive — until you learn that the existing FAQ page was already resolving 60%. The actual impact is 10 percentage points, not 70%. Always measure AI performance against the current baseline, including doing nothing.

## Scaling: From Pilot to Enterprise

Scaling AI is not "doing the pilot bigger." It introduces new challenges:

- **Data diversity** — The pilot operated on data from one department. Scaling to ten departments means ten different data formats, quality levels, and access policies
- **Infrastructure load** — A pilot serving 100 users can run on a single server. Enterprise deployment requires load balancing, redundancy, and monitoring
- **Organizational resistance** — The pilot team volunteered. Scaling means deploying to teams that didn't ask for AI, may not want it, and will need dedicated change management
- **Governance requirements** — Pilots can operate with informal oversight. Enterprise systems need formal governance: access controls, audit logs, bias monitoring, and regulatory compliance

Walmart's approach to scaling AI is instructive. The company runs hundreds of AI pilots simultaneously but has a rigorous stage-gate process for scaling. Each pilot must demonstrate measurable business impact, technical reliability, and operational readiness before receiving approval and budget for enterprise deployment. This discipline means Walmart kills more AI projects than it scales — and the ones that scale work.

> **Key takeaway:** Successful AI implementation requires disciplined problem selection, well-designed pilots with predefined success criteria, ruthless evaluation, and a structured scaling process. Measure AI by business outcomes, not model accuracy. And maintain the organizational courage to kill projects that don't work — that discipline is what separates organizations that extract value from AI from those that just spend money on it.
